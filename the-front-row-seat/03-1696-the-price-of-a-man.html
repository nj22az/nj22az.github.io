Chapter Three

1696

"Five hundred pounds for one man. There are streets in Wapping you could buy for less."

— Broadsheet, London, 1696

1696: The Price of a Man

I. The Paper on the Post

There is a proclamation nailed to the post at the top of the river stairs, and it has been there long enough that the rain has had two goes at it and somebody's knife has had one. It names a man — Henry Every, late master of the ship Fancy, called Long Ben by men who claim to have sailed with him, which is suddenly a great many more men than any one ship could carry. It names a number: five hundred pounds. And between the man and the number it lists his crimes in the careful language of men who were not there — piracy upon the high seas, robbery of the ship Gunsway of Surat, offences against the peace of our sovereign lord the King and the amity of his allies.

What the proclamation does not say, everyone in Wapping already knows, because sailors talk and the story has had a year to cross two oceans and every bar in between. The Gunsway — Ganj-i-Sawai, the men who've been east say, rolling the strangeness of it on their tongues — was the Great Mughal's own ship, the richest thing afloat on any water, coming home from Mecca heavy with gold and pilgrims. Every took her off the Indian coast with one lucky broadside and two hours of boarding work, and what his crew did aboard her in the days that followed is not printed anywhere, and gets told in this bar only late, and quietly, and never with pride. There were women aboard. Some of them went into the sea rather than wait. That part never makes the broadsheets. The gold makes every one of them.

Six hundred thousand pounds, the number goes, though nobody who says it can count that high. And the Emperor of India — who commands more soldiers than England has people, and in whose ports the Company lives on sufferance, like a mouse in a granary — wants the man who did it, and has shut the Company's factories and put its factors in irons until somebody brings him.

So the whole world is looking for one man, on the Crown's proclamation and the Company's silver. The first time, the old hands say, the whole world has ever been asked. And the whole world, a year on, has not found him.

II. Esther Finch

She comes in on Thursday evenings to settle accounts — the bar sends her its overflow when ships pay off and every bed in Wapping fills, and she sends back men who want a hot breakfast, and the difference gets squared over a small beer she makes last an hour. Esther Finch, widow, four lodging rooms two doors up from the stairs. Married twenty years to a ship's carpenter who went to the yard one February morning and came home on a plank, and since then the reckoning of her life has been small and exact: what a room brings in, what a fire costs, what a winter takes out of a roof and out of a woman's hands.

Her upstairs-back is let to a quiet man who calls himself Adams. Paid three months in advance, which nobody does. Polite, keeps no company, mended her stair rail without being asked and without mentioning it after. He sits sometimes at her kitchen table of an evening and watches the door — not nervously, nothing so easy to name — the way a man watches who is keeping a private tally of everyone who comes through it.

He pays his rent in coin she has to carry to three different men before one will change it. Gold, thin and bright, stamped with writing that runs the wrong way and no king's head she knows.

She is not a fool. She reads the proclamation every time she passes the post, the way other people check the sky for weather.

III. The Man Who Was at Bombay

Tanner drinks at the far end most nights, and when the talk turns — as it turns all autumn — to whether the Company will get its factories back, he laughs in a way that has no laughing in it.

He was a soldier at Bombay through the war. The Company's own war: not the King's, the Company's, declared out of a counting-house because a man in London decided the Mughal could be squeezed like a Kentish tenant. Tanner spent it behind the walls of an island fort while the Sidi's army sat outside and the fever sat inside, and he can tell you which of the two killed more Englishmen, and that neither of them was ever once inconvenienced by anything the Company's directors wrote in a letter.

We lost, he says, to anyone who asks and several who don't. Not lost a battle — lost the whole of it, everywhere, at once. And do you know how it ended? Two of our envoys carried to the Emperor's camp, and they went in and prostrated themselves — he says the word carefully, like handling something sharp — flat on the carpet, in front of the whole court, and begged the man's pardon, and paid his fine, and thanked him. And Aurangzeb let the Company back in the way you let a dog back in. Because the trade was useful. Not because the dog was forgiven.

He drinks.

I bled thirteen months for an island the King rents to the Company for ten pound a year. Ten pound. That's Bombay's price, on paper, signed. By the end of the siege I wouldn't have fetched a shilling of it.

And this is the part Tanner wants understood, the reason he tells it at all: the Company knows, better than any man in this bar, that it cannot fight the Mughal. It tried. It ended face-down on a carpet. So now that a pirate has robbed the Emperor's own ship and hurt his own pilgrims, and the Emperor has said bring me the man — the Company will bring him a man. It cannot bring him a war and it cannot bring him a navy, so it will bring him the only thing England produces reliably in all weathers.

Necks, someone says.

Necks, Tanner agrees, and goes back to his pint.

The pint is ale, but the short glass beside it is the new thing: clear spirit, juniper-sharp — Hollands, the house calls it now, or geneva. Since a Dutchman took the English throne it has become nearly a patriotic act to prefer it to brandy, brandy being French, and France being the war. Old men grumble that their fathers called this stuff Dutch physic and wouldn't wash a wound with it. Their sons drink it for King William and the Protestant succession. It is the first time in this room's memory that a drink has changed its nationality. It will not be the last.

It gets its demonstration the same week. A stranger off a Rotherhithe hoy asks for brandy, and the room does a small cold thing it has learned this year: it pauses. Brandy is French; France harbours the men who would put a knife in King William — half of Wapping swore the loyal Association this spring over exactly that — and a man drinking French in a Protestant bar in an assassination year ought at least to have the manners to do it quietly. The stranger reads the silence the way sailors read weather. Hollands, he amends. The room resumes its noise where it left it. Wise, says someone, to nobody in particular, and the keeper pours the juniper and says nothing at all, because a house that survives three reigns learns to sell what the year is drinking.

IV. The Odd Gold

The talk in the bar, the week the news comes up from Bristol, is all about how they're catching them — because they are catching them, a few here, a few there, the Fancy's crew scattered across three kingdoms like spilled shot. And it isn't clever work doing it. No disguises pierced, no confessions won. It's the coin.

You can't drink Mughal gold quietly in England, a waterman explains, with the satisfaction of a man who has thought about this at length on the water. Chequins. Arabian pieces. Gold that shines wrong and reads backward. A man walks into a tavern in Bristol and pays for ale with a coin the tapster's never seen, and the tapster remembers him, and remembers telling somebody, and that somebody remembers the proclamation. It's not the law finding them. It's the change in their pockets, informing.

Esther Finch, who has come in to settle Thursday accounts, goes very still over her small beer.

There is a tin under a floorboard in her kitchen with three of those coins in it, taken in good faith for rent.

She does the reckoning that night, lying awake, because reckoning is what she has instead of prayer. Five hundred pounds. Her whole house — the beds, the linens, the roof her husband mended the summer before he died — might fetch eighty. Her husband earned thirty a year with his hands and they lived decently on it, and were pleased with themselves. Five hundred pounds is not money. It is a different life entirely: no more scrubbing, no more February, a servant girl of her own, a pew where the nod comes to her first. It is every want she has been too sensible to permit herself, stacked in one column, payable on information received.

And in the other column: a quiet man who mended her stair.

And under both columns, where she cannot stop it surfacing, the part of the story that gets told only late and quietly: the women on that ship. If Adams sailed on the Fancy, he was aboard her those days too. Whatever was done, he was at least near enough to hear it. She lies awake with that longer than with the gold.

V. The Trial Run Twice

In October they try six of the Fancy's crew at the Old Bailey, for the taking of the Ganj-i-Sawai, and the jury — London men, merchants and honest tradesmen, no friends of piracy — comes back with not guilty.

The bar hears it the way you hear a wall crack. Not guilty. The government's men are said to be past fury and into something quieter. The Emperor of India has been promised English justice, and English justice has just been asked its opinion and given the wrong one.

So they ask it again.

A different charge the second time — not the Mughal's ship now, but the Fancy herself, stolen from her rightful owners when Every's men rose and sailed her off. Same six men. New jury. And this time the verdict comes back the way verdicts come back when everyone in the room understands what the verdict is for.

That's not a court, the old waterman says, when the news reaches the bar. That's a pair of scales. You go on adding weight to the one side until it balances the way the buyer wants, and then you call it weighing.

In November, at low tide, they hang all six at Execution Dock, on the mud below the stairs, close enough that the bar's windows carry the sound of the crowd in stages — the long murmur, the swell, the silence, the swell again. The bar half-empties for it the way it always does. Esther Finch does not go down. She watches from her upstairs-front window, which her lodgers would pay extra for on a day like this if she were the kind who charged, and she watches the whole of it, and what she carries away is not the choking.

It is this: not one of the six is Every. The man on the proclamation, the man with the number on his head, the man who gave the orders on the Gunsway's deck, is at this moment somewhere the whole world is not, spending his gold slowly or sleeping in a hedge, nobody knows. The Emperor asked for the pirate. He is getting pirates — genuinely, six of them, guilty as rain, nobody says otherwise. But the trade needed six necks by winter, and the first court that looked at the matter honestly said no, and the machine simply asked again until it heard yes. The right man was worth five hundred pounds. Any man, it turns out, was worth just as much to the men who needed the hanging done.

VI. The Asking

He comes in on a Thursday in December, which is her evening, and later she will wonder about that.

Not a constable. Something newer than a constable — a taker of information, retained, he says, on behalf of the owners' interest and other parties concerned, by which everyone in the bar understands the Company, the way you understand the weather without being introduced to it. Good coat for this street. A ledger book he doesn't open, because the not-opening is the point of carrying it.

He works the room without hurry. Any new lodgers since the spring, gentlemen? Any man quiet in his habits, free with his money, careful with his face? Any coin passed lately that read — he smiles at his own delicacy — unusually? The reward stands, he reminds them. Portions payable for any of the crew. The full sum for the captain. Discretion assured, names taken in confidence, payment prompt.

Lodging houses are his best soil and he knows it, and there are only so many within sound of the stairs, and so in time he settles onto the stool across from Esther Finch with the unhurried courtesy of a man who has already read the parish rate-book.

Four rooms, is it, Mrs. Finch? Anyone new since spring? Anyone quiet? Pays regular, does he? In what coin?

And here it is, across the table, asking to be picked up: the servant girl, the warm February, the pew. Five hundred pounds, or some fat portion of it, payable on a sentence of perhaps ten words. Six men are a month dead on the mud two hundred yards from her door, and the machine that hanged them is sitting opposite her with its ledger shut, offering to make her rich.

I keep a poor house, sir, she says. Sailors' widows and a corn-factor's clerk. If any lodger of mine ever paid me in gold, I'd have fainted straight into the river, and you could have fished the coin out of my apron.

The joke is what sells it. He studies her a moment with the flat professional patience of his trade, finds nothing in her face worth a second visit — she has spent thirty years on this street learning to be found unremarkable by men with ledgers — and moves along to the next table. He leaves his direction on a card, in case memory should improve. She uses it that evening to light a candle, and finds it burns no better than any other paper.

Why she does it, she works out slowly, over the following weeks, the way she works out any sum too long for one sitting. Not because she thinks Adams innocent. She doesn't; she has watched his stillness too closely for that. Not mercy, quite. It is the Dock. She stood at her window in November and watched six men choke for the arithmetic of men in better coats — the Emperor required a number, the Company required the Emperor, the court required the verdict, and the rope required necks, and any necks would answer. If she sold Adams, it would not be to justice. Justice was asked its opinion in October, and acquitted them, and was sent back to try again until it confessed. It would be to the machine — and the machine would pay her, promptly and in confidence, and then she would be a woman who had been bought, at a known price, forever.

She has been poor her whole life. She has never yet been purchased. It seems to her, at fifty-one, too late to begin.

And one thing more, which she permits herself only once and then puts away: if she believed for a moment that selling him would answer for the women on the Gunsway — she might do it even so, and scrub the money clean somehow after. But nothing that happened in October suggests any court is asking about the women. Nobody in a coat has ever once asked about the women. The sale would answer for nothing but the price.

VII. What the River Won't Sell

Adams leaves in the spring, the way such men leave — settled to the day, no farewell beyond an extra coin on the kitchen table, the wrong-way writing catching the morning light. She never learns which man he was on that ship, or whether he was on it at all, and she understands by now that this not-knowing is permanent, part of the price of the choice she made, payable in small instalments for the rest of her life.

She takes the coins from the tin under the floorboard, all of them, and walks down to the stairs at low tide, and throws them one at a time as far out over the brown water as a widow's arm can send them. Not a judgment. Not repentance. She simply declines to hold the price, having declined to be paid it. The river takes the gold the way it takes everything, without asking whose it was, and gives back its usual nothing.

Henry Every is never caught. The proclamation on the post weathers to grey lace; boys peel at it; by summer a notice about a dredging contract has gone up over the top of it, and the first worldwide manhunt in the history of the world ends the way most asking ends — not answered, just no longer asked. Somewhere east, the Emperor's ledgers reopen, the factors come out of irons, the fleets sail on schedule, and the Company writes down the whole affair, six necks included, as a cost of trade recovered.

Five years from now the crowd will walk down to the mud again for a bigger name, and the pie-seller will do a better trade, and the man on the ladder that time will have a commission signed near enough by the King himself. Nobody standing at Esther Finch's window will find any of it strange by then. The Dock has been teaching Wapping the same lesson, gently and often, for as long as the tide has run: when the trade requires a body, a body will be found. The only question the river has ever left open is whose.
